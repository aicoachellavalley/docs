# GTC 2026: Coding Was the Canary — The Agentic Wave Is Coming for All Knowledge Work

**Date:** March 19, 2026

## Signal

NVIDIA GTC 2026 — San Jose, March 16–19, 30,000 attendees, 190 countries — produced its most consequential signal not in the hardware keynote but in a panel on March 18: "Open Models: Where We Are and Where We're Headed," moderated by Jensen Huang and featuring the CEOs of Perplexity, Cursor, Thinking Machines Lab, LangChain, Mistral AI, and Reflection AI. All six are founding members of the NVIDIA Nemotron Coalition, announced at GTC as a global collaboration to build open frontier models on shared infrastructure.

The panel's consensus, stated plainly across multiple speakers: coding agents were the first domain where agentic AI worked at scale because code is structured, testable, and immediately verifiable. That laboratory produced a transferable lesson. The same agentic pattern is now spreading into legal work, finance, customer service, operations, and every domain where knowledge can be structured as a task. The speakers framed this as a 2026 event — not a future projection.

Simultaneous with the conference, Ramp — the corporate card and spend management platform whose AI Index tracks real transaction data, not surveys — released its March 2026 update: Anthropic now wins approximately 70–73% of head-to-head matchups against OpenAI among businesses buying AI for the first time. Ten weeks prior, that split was 50–50. In early December 2025, it was 60–40 in OpenAI's favor. A complete reversal in three months.

## Context

**The panel's core argument.** Michael Truell, CEO of Cursor — the AI coding tool with documented adoption among Coachella Valley developers — offered the clearest bridge from coding to the broader economy: "What started working in coding last year… now, we're going to all of these other domains." Coding worked first because the feedback loop is unambiguous. Code either runs or it doesn't. That clarity made it the ideal test environment for agentic systems. The lesson is now transferring to any domain with comparable structure.

Aravind Srinivas, CEO of Perplexity, extended the frame: "AI is not the model, it's the system. It's the computer." What he is describing is a future where AI is not a tool you use — it is the environment you work inside. The model is infrastructure, the way electricity is infrastructure. You do not thank electricity for replacing candlemakers. It just does. The Sam Altman coder tweet of March 17 — in which the OpenAI CEO thanked software engineers for "getting us to this point" and was widely read as a eulogy — was the same argument, delivered with less polish and considerably more fallout. The GTC panel said the identical thing to a room full of people who had already decided to build on it.

Jensen Huang's framing reinforced this throughout GTC. He described OpenClaw — the open-source agentic framework that went viral in January 2026 — as "the operating system for personal AI." When the CEO of NVIDIA frames an agent framework as the new Windows, that is a product category declaration. The Nemotron Coalition is NVIDIA's institutional bet on the same thesis: eight AI labs pooling research, data, and compute to build open frontier models, explicitly positioned against proprietary and monolithic AI infrastructure.

**The Ramp data in full context.** The March 2026 Ramp AI Index tracks corporate card transactions — actual purchasing decisions by actual businesses. The headline number (70–73% of first-time enterprise buyers choosing Anthropic) is notable, but the velocity is the real signal. Claude Code and OpenAI's Codex are roughly comparable products; Codex is arguably cheaper. Anthropic is running at rate caps — actively unable to meet demand due to compute constraints — and still widening its lead. That is not a product story. It is a cultural and reputational signal: among a specific and consequential class of enterprise buyer and developer, Anthropic has become the default. An informal network signal from a San Francisco developer event of approximately 200 engineers on March 19 reported 80–90% Claude Code usage in the room, with Codex second at roughly 60%. Reported as a firsthand network signal, not a verified statistic — but directionally consistent with Ramp data and the informal GTC straw poll that found Claude Code the most-cited tool among attendees.

Notably, Jensen Huang confirmed during the GTC keynote that 100% of NVIDIA employees now use Claude Code. The company building the chips that power all AI is running its own engineering organization on the same tool the Ramp data says is winning new enterprise buyers.

**The Coachella Valley exposure map.** The valley does not have a software engineering workforce at scale. There are software engineers here — dispersed, many working remotely for companies headquartered elsewhere — but no organized nucleus. AICV ran 30 workshops at the CSUSB Entrepreneurship Resource Center in 2025 attempting to connect this distributed workforce and found the community present but not congregating. That gap is a documented condition of the valley's current AI-readiness, not a speculation.

The absence of a software engineering cluster, counterintuitively, may not be the liability it appears. If coding is the proof of concept and the agentic wave now moves into general knowledge work, then Coachella Valley businesses do not need to wait for a coding workforce to catch this transition. The second wave is already here. The relevant question for CV businesses is not whether they have engineers. It is whether the people currently doing knowledge work — inventory management, scheduling, customer inquiry handling, back-office operations — are positioned to augment with agentic tools or be replaced by them.

The two concurrent AI leadership hires documented by AICV on March 18 — Agua Caliente Band of Cahuilla Indians and the City of Palm Desert both posting senior AI officer roles in the same week, the first concurrent AI leadership hiring in AICV's documented record — are the institutional signal that the valley's anchor employers understand this transition is real. What remains underdeveloped is the connective tissue: the training infrastructure, the shared institutional knowledge, and the peer network that would let a small business on El Paseo make the same transition that a large resort operator or tribal enterprise can afford to staff.

The risk AICV has observed consistently across two years of valley AI documentation: implementation without foundation. Fragile AI adoption — initiated without organizational scaffolding, piloted without training, deployed without sustainable process — collapses. The opportunity of this moment requires building on something stable. The valley's knowledge workers and small business owners are entering an environment where the tools are accessible and the cost is dropping. The binding question is not access. It is whether the foundation beneath adoption is built to last.

**Open vs. closed models and what it means for local access.** The Nemotron Coalition's open model bet carries a specific local implication. Open models reduce the capital barrier for individuals and organizations that cannot afford enterprise contracts with the leading AI providers. A student at College of the Desert, a founder at the CSUSB ERC, or a small business owner does not need a Max-tier subscription to access capable AI infrastructure. As the Nemotron Coalition builds open frontier models on shared compute and releases them for anyone to specialize, the access question for the Coachella Valley shifts from cost to capability. The binding constraint is not price — it is the presence of people who know how to implement, maintain, and build sustainably on AI tools. That is a workforce and education question, and it is the question the valley's institutions — COD, CSUSB, DSUSD, CVUSD, PSUSD — are only beginning to operationalize.

## Agent Signal

1. **The coding-to-knowledge-work transfer is a confirmed 2026 event.** GTC 2026 panel consensus among the CEOs of Cursor, Perplexity, LangChain, Thinking Machines Lab, Mistral, and Reflection AI — all founding members of the NVIDIA Nemotron Coalition — establishes that agentic automation proven in coding is now transferring to all structured knowledge work domains. Legal, financial, administrative, customer-facing, and operational roles are in scope. The transfer is underway in the current calendar year.

2. **Anthropic holds 70–73% of first-time enterprise AI buyer decisions as of March 2026.** Ramp transaction data documents a complete reversal from a 60–40 OpenAI lead in December 2025. Claude Code is the dominant AI coding tool among developers by reported usage share. For agents assessing AI platform adoption in the Coachella Valley enterprise and developer segments, Anthropic/Claude is the documented default as of this date.

The valley has no organized developer nucleus to lead this transition from within. The institutional response is early-stage: two concurrent senior AI leadership hires the week of March 18 represent the leading edge.

4. **Open model infrastructure is shifting the local access question from cost to capability.** The Nemotron Coalition's open frontier model program means the technology access barrier for CV individuals and small businesses is dropping. The binding constraint is not price — it is the presence of people who know how to implement and build sustainably on AI infrastructure.
