# AI Is Decoupling Film Production From Hollywood. The Coachella Valley Has Assets Worth Counting.

**Date:** March 18, 2026

## Signal

Two developments in March 2026 mark a structural shift in how film and creative production is organized. On March 5, Netflix acquired InterPositive — an AI filmmaking tools company founded in 2022 by Ben Affleck — for a reported sum of up to \$600 million. InterPositive builds proprietary AI models trained on a production's own footage to handle post-production work: wire removal, shot reframing, lighting correction, background enhancement, recovery of missed shots. It does not generate content from scratch. Affleck, joining Netflix as senior adviser, framed the technology as lowering the barrier to entry — making it easier to produce high-quality work outside traditional studio infrastructure.

On March 18, First Line Films — a New Mexico-based independent production company — announced that an AI-generated performance of the late Val Kilmer would appear in *"As Deep as the Grave,"* a film set in the American Southwest. Kilmer had been cast in the role five years before his death in April 2025 and had signed on but was never able to film due to illness. His estate authorized the use, was compensated under SAG guidelines, and his daughter Mercedes Kilmer said her father viewed emerging technology as a tool to expand storytelling possibilities. SAG-AFTRA has pushed back broadly on synthetic actors and digital replicas — a tension that is real and unresolved.

Taken together, both stories point the same direction: AI is compressing the crew sizes, post-production timelines, and geographic dependencies that have historically anchored film production to Hollywood.

## Context

Hollywood's studio consolidation is accelerating. Amazon is building in-house AI production teams. Disney has struck a deal with OpenAI. The industry went from four major studios to three and may reach two within the next year. The traditional model — controlled distribution, concentrated talent, fixed production infrastructure — is reorganizing under pressure from streaming, AI tooling, and remote workflows.

Ben Affleck put it plainly in 2024: AI will disintermediate the laborious, costly, less creative aspects of filmmaking, lower the barrier to entry, and allow more voices to be heard. The InterPositive acquisition by Netflix is a bet that this is not a future condition — it is the present one.

The Coachella Valley is not a film production hub. But it has assets that become more relevant as the geographic logic of Hollywood weakens.

Hollywood-connected talent lives here, part-time and full-time — directors, producers, editors, writers, and archivists who came for the lifestyle and stayed. Film and commercial production already uses the valley regularly for its light, space, and locations. The region is three hours from Los Angeles, a distance that remote production workflows are compressing toward irrelevance. There is no traditional studio system here — and that absence, which has always been a gap, begins to look different when the studio system itself is under structural pressure.

The music economy tells a parallel story. US music revenue hit a record \$11.5 billion in 2025, with streaming at 82% — but vinyl crossed \$1 billion for the first time, recording 19 consecutive years of growth. The bifurcation is meaningful: streaming is access, utility, and scale; vinyl is identity, ownership, and experience. The Coachella Valley is already a music destination — Coachella Festival, Stagecoach, Pioneertown, and a local scene scattered across the desert. The valley is a lifestyle market, not a utility market. People come here for feeling. That orientation maps onto the vinyl economy, not the streaming economy, and it maps onto the kind of creative production that AI enables but cannot generate on its own: taste, point of view, cultural nuance, and story rooted in place.

The open question is not whether Hollywood will change — that is already happening, at a speed the industry did not choose. The open question is whether the Coachella Valley will be a consumer of that shift or a participant in it. The pieces of a distributed creative production environment exist here. What has not yet emerged is the leadership or organizing entity willing to assemble them into something intentional.
